GEOCHEMISTRY

The Carbon Isotope Excursion
at the Ediacaran–Cambrian
Boundary Explained

DATEMARCH 2025
READ TIME~12 MINUTES
AUTHORION FRANCOVSCHI
TOPICCHEMOSTRATIGRAPHY

Imagine standing at an outcrop in Podolia, Oman, Newfoundland, or South China — four places separated by thousands of kilometres and different geological histories — and finding the same geochemical signal inscribed in the carbonate rocks at the same stratigraphic level. A sharp, unmistakeable drop in the ratio of carbon-13 to carbon-12. A negative excursion in δ¹³C that plunges to values as low as −8 or even −10 per mille, then slowly recovers into the Cambrian.

This signal — known as the Basal Cambrian Carbon isotope Excursion, or BACE — is one of the most striking chemostratigraphic markers in the entire geological record. It is found on virtually every palaeocontinent that preserves carbonate-bearing Ediacaran–Cambrian boundary sections. It has been measured in thousands of samples across dozens of sections. And despite decades of study, its ultimate cause remains actively debated.

"A signal recorded simultaneously on every continent is not a local curiosity — it is a message from the global ocean, written in carbon."

— CHEMOSTRATIGRAPHY, FIELD NOTES

THE BASICS: WHAT IS δ¹³C AND WHY DOES IT MATTER?

Carbon has two stable isotopes: carbon-12 (¹²C, the common one, ~99% of all carbon) and carbon-13 (¹³C, ~1%). The ratio between them, expressed as δ¹³C in units of per mille (‰) relative to a standard, records the balance of carbon cycling in the ocean–atmosphere system at the time carbonates were precipitated.

The key process driving δ¹³C in the ocean is organic carbon burial. Photosynthesising organisms preferentially incorporate ¹²C into their tissues, leaving seawater enriched in ¹³C. When organic matter is buried in sediments rather than oxidised back to CO₂, it removes ¹²C from circulation — driving seawater δ¹³C upward (more positive). Conversely, when organic carbon is oxidised or when large amounts of isotopically light carbon enter the ocean, δ¹³C drops.

This is why a large negative excursion is so significant: it records a major perturbation to the global carbon cycle — a sudden influx of light carbon, a collapse of organic carbon burial, or both.

FIG. 1 — Simplified carbon cycle. Organic carbon burial drives seawater δ¹³C positive by removing ¹²C. Input of isotopically light carbon (e.g. from methane hydrates, oxidation of organic matter) drives δ¹³C negative — producing excursions like the BACE.

THE BACE: ANATOMY OF THE EXCURSION

The BACE begins in the uppermost Ediacaran, typically in the last one to two million years before the formal Cambrian boundary at 538.8 Ma. In well-preserved sections, the excursion is characterised by a relatively rapid descent from near-zero or mildly positive δ¹³C values down to a pronounced negative minimum — often between −4 and −8 ‰, with some sections recording values below −10 ‰.

The recovery is generally gradual, stretching across the earliest Cambrian (Terreneuvian epoch), before δ¹³C stabilises at broadly positive values in the Cambrian Series 2. The entire excursion, from onset to full recovery, likely spans several million years.

WHAT CAUSED IT? THE MAIN HYPOTHESES

This is where consensus dissolves. Several mechanisms have been proposed, and the real answer is likely a combination of more than one. Here are the leading hypotheses.

1. Collapse of organic carbon burial

If the organisms responsible for exporting organic carbon to the seafloor suddenly declined in productivity or abundance, less ¹²C would be removed from the ocean. Seawater would become progressively enriched in light carbon, driving δ¹³C negative. The Ediacaran–Cambrian boundary coincides with the disappearance of the Ediacaran macrobiota — large, soft-bodied organisms whose ecological role is still debated. Some researchers argue their demise reduced the biological carbon pump efficiency.

2. Oxidation of a large dissolved organic carbon reservoir

The Neoproterozoic ocean may have harboured an enormous reservoir of dissolved organic carbon — perhaps 10 to 100 times larger than today's. If ocean oxygenation increased significantly at the Ediacaran–Cambrian boundary, this reservoir could have been oxidised, releasing vast quantities of isotopically light CO₂ into seawater and driving δ¹³C sharply negative. This hypothesis, associated with the work of Don Canfield and colleagues, has attracted considerable support.

3. Methane hydrate destabilisation

Methane (CH₄) is extremely depleted in ¹³C (δ¹³C values as low as −60 ‰). If large quantities of methane were released from seafloor hydrates — triggered by ocean warming, sea level change, or tectonic activity — it could drive a rapid and severe negative δ¹³C excursion. This mechanism has been invoked for other Neoproterozoic excursions (notably the SHURAM excursion at ~560 Ma) but its role specifically at the Ediacaran–Cambrian boundary is less well-constrained.

4. Diagenetic overprinting

A more uncomfortable possibility is that some or all of the BACE is not a primary seawater signal at all, but a product of post-depositional alteration. Carbonate rocks are susceptible to diagenesis — recrystallisation, replacement, and exchange with pore fluids that can shift δ¹³C significantly. Careful petrographic screening and the use of multiple geochemical proxies (δ¹⁸O, Mn/Sr ratios, cathodoluminescence) are essential to evaluate the primary nature of any δ¹³C record.

"Before you interpret a carbon isotope signal, you must earn the right to do so — by demonstrating that what you are measuring is what the ancient ocean actually recorded."

— A LESSON FROM EVERY CHEMOSTRATIGRAPHY COURSE
► KEY TERMS
BACE Basal Cambrian Carbon isotope Excursion — the large negative δ¹³C anomaly straddling the Ediacaran–Cambrian boundary, recorded globally in carbonate rocks.
δ¹³C (delta 13C) The ratio of ¹³C to ¹²C in a sample relative to a standard (VPDB), expressed in per mille (‰). Positive values = enriched in ¹³C; negative = depleted.
Organic carbon burial The sequestration of biologically produced organic matter in seafloor sediments rather than its oxidation. Drives seawater δ¹³C positive by removing light carbon.
DOC reservoir Dissolved Organic Carbon pool in seawater. The Neoproterozoic ocean may have held a large, isotopically light DOC pool whose oxidation contributed to negative excursions.
Diagenesis Post-depositional chemical and physical alteration of sedimentary rocks. Can significantly modify the original geochemical signal recorded at time of deposition.
VPDB Vienna Pee Dee Belemnite — the international reference standard used to report stable carbon and oxygen isotope ratios in carbonates.

THE BACE IN SOUTHWESTERN BALTICA

In the Podolian sections of the East European Platform, the BACE is recorded in carbonate-bearing intervals within the uppermost Vendian succession. The signal is not as dramatically expressed as in the Oman or South China sections — partly because the carbonate content of the predominantly siliciclastic Podolian sequence is lower, reducing the number of measurable samples — but the negative trend is recognisable and correlatable.

Extracting a reliable δ¹³C record from Podolia requires careful sample selection. The most suitable material is micritic limestone or dolostone with low Mn/Sr ratios and well-preserved oxygen isotope values, indicating minimal post-depositional alteration. Coarsely recrystallised sparite and veined material are avoided, as are samples with anomalously heavy δ¹⁸O values that suggest meteoric diagenesis.

CORRELATION AND GLOBAL SIGNIFICANCE

The power of the BACE as a stratigraphic tool lies in its global reproducibility. Because it is recorded on virtually every palaeocontinent, it provides one of the few truly worldwide correlation horizons for the Ediacaran–Cambrian boundary interval — a period where biostratigraphic tools are limited by the scarcity and provincialism of Ediacaran fossils.

When combined with radiometric ages (where available), Sr isotope stratigraphy, and biostratigraphic constraints, the BACE allows geologists to correlate sections across oceans and between continents with a precision that would be otherwise impossible in pre-Cambrian rocks. For the EEP, where radiometric dating of sedimentary intervals is limited, the BACE serves as a critical anchor point for correlating the Podolian succession with better-dated sections in Oman, South China, and Laurentia.

What this ultimately tells us is profound: the carbon cycle perturbation recorded by the BACE was not a regional phenomenon. It was a global ocean event — one that coincided with the extinction of the Ediacaran biota, the onset of the Cambrian Explosion, and a major reorganisation of the Earth system. Whether the carbon cycle disruption caused these biological changes, or whether it was a symptom of them, remains one of the most compelling open questions in the geosciences.
